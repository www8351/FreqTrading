###### The algorithmic day trading methodology, as described in the sources, is built on the fundamental premise that the market is not driven by random supply and demand or the collective decisions of retail traders, but by pre-programmed code and algorithms

###### . The primary goal of the algorithm is to seek out and collect liquidity—specifically the stop-loss orders of the public—moving from one external liquidity pool to another

###### .

###### The methodology is divided into several key pillars:

###### 1\. Time Analysis: The Quarters Theory

###### Time is the central driver that activates the algorithm

###### . According to this methodology, every unit of time (year, month, week, day, or session) is divided into four quarters, each with a specific algorithmic role

###### :

###### Quarter 1 (Q1) - Accumulation: Characterized by sideways movement and the creation of liquidity zones

###### .

###### Quarter 2 (Q2) - Manipulation: A movement designed to mislead traders in the wrong direction and hit stop-losses

###### .

###### Quarter 3 (Q3) - Distribution: The "true" and strong movement of the cycle where orders are realized; this is the optimal window for trading

###### .

###### Quarter 4 (Q4) - Reversal or Continuation: Either closing positions or continuing the trend established in Q3

###### .

###### A critical concept is the True Open, which is the opening price of the second quarter (Q2) of any cycle

###### . This price represents "fair value"; trading above it is considered Premium (expensive—look for shorts), and trading below it is Discount (cheap—look for longs)
